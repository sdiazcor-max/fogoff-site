Covers both the Core exam (required for everyone) and Category 7B: Ornamental & Turf (your specific license). You must pass both. Click any section to expand.

FIFRA — Federal Law

The Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act (FIFRA) is the primary federal law governing pesticide use in the United States. Administered by the EPA.

  • All pesticides sold in the US must be registered with the EPA
  • Sets standards for labeling, safety testing, and approved uses
  • Establishes categories for General Use Pesticides (GUP) and Restricted Use Pesticides (RUP)
  • RUPs can only be purchased and applied by certified applicators
  • States can have stricter laws than FIFRA but not weaker

NC Pesticide Law (NCDA&CS)

In North Carolina, the NC Department of Agriculture & Consumer Services (NCDA&CS) administers pesticide licensing through the Structural Pest Control & Pesticides Division.

  • Anyone applying pesticides for compensation on someone else's property must be licensed
  • Private applicators: apply pesticides on their own land for agricultural purposes
  • Commercial applicators: apply pesticides for hire (that's you)
  • You must pass the Core exam + at least one Category exam
  • Category 7B = Ornamental & Turf
  • Records of RUP applications must be kept for at least 2 years
⚠️ The label is the LAW. Following label directions is legally required, not optional.

What Must Be On Every Label

  • Product/brand name
  • Active ingredients (with percentages) and inert ingredients
  • EPA Registration Number and Establishment Number
  • Net contents (weight or volume)
  • Signal word and skull-and-crossbones (if required)
  • First aid / emergency treatment
  • Precautionary statements (hazards to humans, animals, environment)
  • Directions for use (including pests, sites, rates, timing, REI)
  • Storage and disposal instructions

Reading the Label for Application

  • Always read the label before mixing, loading, or applying
  • Directions are site-specific — you can only apply to sites listed on the label
  • Never exceed labeled rates
  • Observe labeled Restricted Entry Intervals (REIs)
  • Restricted Use Pesticides are labeled "RESTRICTED USE PESTICIDE" at top

Most Common Exposure Route

DERMAL (skin) is the #1 exposure route for pesticide applicators. More pesticide enters the body through skin contact than through breathing or swallowing. Chemical-resistant gloves are your most critical PPE.

Routes of Exposure (in order of frequency)

  1. Dermal (skin) — most common; often unnoticed
  2. Inhalation (lungs) — especially in enclosed spaces or with fine sprays
  3. Oral (swallowing) — often from hand-to-mouth; wash hands!
  4. Ocular (eyes) — eyes absorb rapidly; splash risk

Required PPE (varies by label)

  • Chemical-resistant gloves — always (not just leather or fabric)
  • Long-sleeved shirt and long pants — minimum for most labels
  • Closed-toe shoes + socks — no sandals
  • Protective eyewear — goggles or face shield for concentrates
  • Respirator — when label requires (N95 for particulates; OV cartridge for organic vapors/fumigants)
  • Chemical-resistant apron — when mixing concentrates

Critical Safety Rules

  • Never eat, drink, smoke, or use tobacco while applying pesticides
  • Wash hands and face before eating, drinking, or using the restroom
  • Never wear contaminated clothing home — change and wash separately
  • Remove PPE carefully (peel gloves inside-out) to avoid self-contamination
  • Shower and change clothes as soon as possible after application
☎️ Poison Control: 1-800-222-1222 — memorize this number.

LD50

Lethal Dose 50 (LD50) is the dose of a chemical that kills 50% of a test population. Expressed in mg of pesticide per kg of body weight.

⚠️ Lower LD50 = MORE toxic. An LD50 of 5 mg/kg is far more dangerous than 500 mg/kg.

Key Pesticide Classes & Symptoms

Organophosphates & Carbamates

Cholinesterase inhibitors. Symptoms: SLUDGE — Salivation, Lacrimation (tears), Urination, Defecation, GI distress, Emesis (vomiting). Also: miosis (pinpoint pupils), muscle tremors.

Pyrethrins & Pyrethroids

Sodium channel disruptors. Symptoms: paresthesia (tingling/burning skin), tremors, seizures in severe cases. Generally less toxic to mammals.

Organochlorines

CNS stimulants. Most banned (DDT). Symptoms: tremors, convulsions. Bioaccumulate in fatty tissue.

Neonicotinoids

Nicotinic receptor agonists. Systemic in plants. High bee toxicity — avoid applying to blooming flowers.

First Aid Basics

  • Skin contact: Remove contaminated clothing, flush with water 15–20 min
  • Eye contact: Flush with clean water 15–20 min; seek medical attention
  • Ingestion: Do NOT induce vomiting unless label says to; call 1-800-222-1222
  • Inhalation: Move to fresh air; if not breathing, call 911
  • Always bring the pesticide label to the emergency room

What is IPM?

IPM is an ecosystem-based strategy that uses a combination of practices to manage pests. Chemical control is a last resort — not the first step.

IPM Steps (in order)

  1. Identify the pest correctly — don't guess
  2. Monitor and assess — how many? where? how fast spreading?
  3. Set action threshold — at what population level does control make economic sense?
  4. Prevent — cultural and mechanical controls first
  5. Control — biological → cultural → mechanical → chemical (last resort)
  6. Evaluate — did it work? adjust next time

Key Thresholds

  • Economic Injury Level (EIL): Pest population that causes economic damage equal to control cost. Never let pest exceed this.
  • Economic Threshold (ET) / Action Threshold: Population level at which you should ACT to prevent reaching EIL. Always below EIL.
💡 ET < EIL. You act at the ET to prevent reaching the EIL.

Types of Pest Control

  • Biological: Predators, parasitoids, pathogens (Bt, beneficial nematodes)
  • Cultural: Mowing, irrigation timing, fertilization, crop rotation, resistant varieties
  • Mechanical/Physical: Traps, barriers, hand removal
  • Chemical: Pesticides — most effective but environmental risk

What is WPS?

WPS is an EPA regulation that protects agricultural workers and pesticide handlers from pesticide exposure on farms, forests, nurseries, and greenhouses.

Restricted Entry Intervals (REI)

  • The REI is the time after a pesticide application during which no one can enter the treated area without required PPE
  • REI is listed on every pesticide label
  • Minimum REI is 4 hours for most pesticides; can be much longer
  • Workers entering during REI (early entry) must wear specific PPE

Employer Requirements

  • Post warning signs at entries to treated areas during REI
  • Provide safety training to all agricultural workers
  • Provide decontamination supplies (water, soap, towels) near treated area
  • Provide access to pesticide application information (central posting)
  • Provide emergency medical transportation if needed
📌 REI = how long to stay OUT. Pre-harvest interval (PHI) = how long before you can HARVEST after application.

Key Formula

GPA = (GPM × 5,940) ÷ (MPH × Nozzle Spacing in inches)

Where: GPA = Gallons Per Acre · GPM = Gallons Per Minute output · MPH = Speed

Mixing Concentrates

  • Always add water first, then pesticide when mixing (fill tank halfway, add pesticide, fill to top)
  • Always measure accurately — never guess rates
  • If label says "2 oz per gallon" and you need 5 gallons: 2 × 5 = 10 oz of product

Important Conversions

  • 1 acre = 43,560 sq ft
  • 1 gallon = 128 fluid oz
  • 1 pound = 16 oz
  • 1 tablespoon = 3 teaspoons = 0.5 fl oz

Calibration Check

To check output: catch nozzle output for 30 seconds, multiply by 2 = GPM. Compare to desired rate.

Pesticide Drift

  • Drift = movement of pesticides off-target through air
  • Use larger droplets (low-pressure, larger nozzle orifice) to minimize drift
  • Apply in low wind (3–10 mph ideal; >15 mph stop)
  • Apply early morning or evening (less wind, less heat volatilization)
  • Never apply when rain is imminent

Water Protection

  • Observe buffer zones near water bodies listed on label
  • Never apply to or near storm drains, ditches, or ponds
  • Pesticides most likely to contaminate groundwater: high water solubility, low soil adsorption, persistent
  • Prevent back-siphoning when filling tanks (use check valve or air gap)

Endangered Species Act

Federal law making it illegal to kill, harm, harass, or collect endangered or threatened species. Some pesticide labels include Endangered Species Protection Bulletins — check these before applying.

🐝 Pollinators (honeybees) are especially vulnerable to insecticides. Apply when bees are not foraging (evening) and avoid applying to blooming plants.

Cool-Season Grasses (northern NC)

  • Tall Fescue — most common cool-season in NC; bunch-type; deep roots; drought tolerant
  • Kentucky Bluegrass — spreading rhizomes; excellent quality; less heat tolerant
  • Perennial Ryegrass — rapid germination; used for overseeding; no rhizomes
  • Grow best in fall and spring; go dormant or struggle in NC summer heat

Warm-Season Grasses (Charlotte/southern NC)

  • Bermudagrass — most common warm-season; aggressive; stolons + rhizomes; invades flowerbeds
  • Zoysiagrass — slow growing; dense; good shade tolerance; wiry texture
  • Centipedegrass — low maintenance; light green; low fertility needs; stolons only
  • St. Augustinegrass — coarse texture; excellent shade tolerance; stolons; not cold-hardy
  • Grow best in summer; go dormant (brown) in winter
📍 Charlotte is in the Piedmont — transition zone. Both warm and cool-season grasses are grown here. Bermuda and tall fescue are most common in 28205.

Turfgrass Problem Diagnosis Steps

  1. Identify the grass species
  2. Look at distribution (uniform vs. irregular pattern)
  3. Check roots, crowns, and soil
  4. Consider recent weather and management practices
  5. Determine if pest, disease, or cultural/environmental cause

Root-Feeding Insects

White Grubs

Larvae of Japanese beetles, masked chafers, May/June beetles. C-shaped, creamy-white. Feed on roots → turf lifts like carpet. Treat late summer when young grubs are near surface.

Mole Crickets

Tunnel through soil, severing roots. Mounds of loose soil visible. Mostly bermuda and bahia. Flush test: soapy water flush reveals mole crickets if present.

Billbugs

Adults are weevils; larvae feed in crown and roots. Turf pulls up easily in fall. "Sawdust" frass at crown. Zoysiagrass vulnerable.

Surface-Feeding Insects

Sod Webworm

Caterpillars of small moths. Chew off grass blades near soil surface. Irregular brown patches. Birds pecking is a sign. Soap flush test reveals larvae.

Armyworm (Fall)

Fall armyworm can strip turf overnight. Greenish-brown caterpillars. Look for egg masses on light-colored surfaces near turf. Bermuda and ryegrass most attacked.

Chinch Bugs

Piercing-sucking insects. Cause yellowing patches in hot, dry areas (full sun). Mostly St. Augustinegrass. Flotation method: remove can bottom, push into soil, fill with water — chinch bugs float up.

Fire Ants

Build mounds 1–2 ft tall. Sting in mass when disturbed. Two-step control: broadcast bait + individual mound treatment. Never disturb mound before applying bait.

🔍 Soap flush test: 1–2 tbsp dish soap in 1 gallon water, pour on 1 sq ft of turf. Surface insects emerge in 2–5 minutes.

Common NC Turfgrass Diseases

Brown Patch (Rhizoctonia)

Most common NC turf disease. Circular brown patches with "smoke ring" border. Tall fescue and bermuda. Triggered by hot, humid nights (>70°F). Reduce nitrogen, improve drainage.

Dollar Spot (Sclerotinia)

Small (silver-dollar sized) bleached spots. White mycelium visible in morning dew. Indicates low nitrogen. Improve fertility, reduce dew period. Fungicide: propiconazole.

Pythium Blight

Greasy, water-soaked patches that collapse rapidly. White cottony mycelium at margins. Worst in hot, wet conditions with poor drainage. Pythium is an oomycete (water mold), not true fungus — requires oomycete-active fungicides (mefenoxam).

Spring Dead Spot

Circular dead patches in bermudagrass that fail to green up in spring. Caused by Ophiosphaerella spp. Apply fungicide in fall before dormancy. Avoid high nitrogen in late summer.

Gray Leaf Spot

Tan/gray diamond-shaped lesions on leaves. Primarily St. Augustinegrass and perennial ryegrass. Worse in hot, humid summer. Reduce nitrogen; apply fungicide preventively.

Red Thread / Pink Patch

Pink or red thread-like mycelium visible in turf. Cool, wet weather. Low nitrogen indicator. Improve fertility. Fungicide if severe.

Fungicide Resistance Management

  • Rotate between FRAC groups (different modes of action)
  • FRAC 11 (azoxystrobin, pyraclostrobin) — most common; high resistance risk
  • FRAC 3 (propiconazole, triticonazole) — DMI fungicides
  • FRAC 7 (fluopyram, boscalid) — SDHI fungicides
  • Tank-mix or rotate; never use same FRAC group consecutively

Weed Categories

By Lifecycle

Annual: complete lifecycle in one year (summer annuals: crabgrass; winter annuals: annual bluegrass/Poa annua)
Perennial: live multiple years, regrow from roots (dandelion, nutsedge)

By Type

Grassy weeds: crabgrass, goosegrass, annual bluegrass, nutsedge (sedge)
Broadleaf weeds: dandelion, clover, spurge, chickweed, henbit, oxalis

Herbicide Timing

  • Pre-emergent: Apply BEFORE weed germination. Prevents seedling establishment. Common: pendimethalin, prodiamine, dithiopyr. Apply before soil temps reach 55°F (crabgrass) in spring.
  • Post-emergent: Apply AFTER weeds are visible. Works on actively growing weeds.

Herbicide Selectivity

  • Selective: Kill specific plants (e.g., 2,4-D kills broadleaf but not grass)
  • Non-selective: Kill all plants (glyphosate/Roundup, triclopyr at high rates)

Common Herbicide Modes of Action

  • Photosynthesis inhibitors: atrazine, simazine
  • Growth regulators (synthetic auxins): 2,4-D, dicamba, triclopyr — broadleaf killers
  • Amino acid synthesis inhibitors: glyphosate (EPSPS inhibitor)
  • Cell membrane disruptors: paraquat, carfentrazone
⚠️ Nutsedge (yellow and purple) is a sedge, not a grass — standard grass or broadleaf herbicides often don't work. Requires sedge-specific products (sulfentrazone, halosulfuron).

Key Ornamental Insect Pests

Aphids

Soft-bodied, piercing-sucking. Cluster on new growth. Produce honeydew → sooty mold. Many natural predators (ladybugs, lacewings). Soap spray effective.

Scale Insects

Armored scale: hard cover; contact insecticides don't penetrate — use systemic or horticultural oil during crawler stage.
Soft scale: waxy; easier to control; produce honeydew.

Spider Mites

NOT insects — 8-legged arachnids. Stippled, bronze leaves; fine webbing. Worse in hot, dry conditions. Many insecticides make them WORSE (kill predatory mites). Use miticide/acaricide.

Whiteflies

Tiny, white; fly in clouds when plant is disturbed. Piercing-sucking; honeydew and sooty mold. Apply insecticide to leaf undersides. Systemic insecticides (imidacloprid) effective.

Thrips

Rasping-sucking. Cause silvery streaks, distorted flowers. Especially on roses and impatiens. Spinosad effective; rotate with other modes of action.

Borers

Larvae bore into stems/trunks. Sawdust-like frass at entry holes. Often target stressed trees. Prevent by keeping plants healthy. Systemic insecticides applied preventively; hard to kill once inside.

🕷️ Mites are NOT insects — they are arachnids (8 legs). Do not treat with standard insecticides; use a miticide/acaricide. This is a common exam question.

Ornamental Diseases

  • Powdery mildew: White powdery coating on leaves; loves dry weather + humid nights; use fungicide (tebuconazole, myclobutanil)
  • Black spot (roses): Black circular spots with yellow halos on leaves; requires wet weather; preventive fungicide program
  • Fire blight: Bacterial disease of apple/pear family; "shepherd's crook" dieback; prune 12" below infection; copper bactericide
  • Root rot (Phytophthora): Oomycete; poorly drained soils; requires oomycete-active fungicide (mefenoxam)

Mosquito Biology

  • Complete metamorphosis: egg → larva → pupa → adult (4 stages)
  • Eggs require standing water to hatch (even 1/4 inch)
  • Larval and pupal stages are aquatic
  • Only female mosquitoes bite (need blood for egg development)
  • Primary NC species: Aedes albopictus (Asian tiger, daytime biter), Culex quinquefasciatus (house mosquito, evening)

Control Methods — IPM Approach

  • Source reduction (best): Eliminate standing water — key breeding sites: gutters, pots, birdbaths, low spots, tarps
  • Larviciding: BTi (Bacillus thuringiensis israelensis) — biological, safe for humans/pets/wildlife. Mosquito dunks. Apply to standing water.
  • Adulticiding (what you do): ULV cold fogging with pyrethrin-based products

Pyrethrin Products (Relevant to Fog Off)

  • Pyrethrins — derived from chrysanthemum flowers; fast knockdown; breaks down quickly in UV light; relatively low mammalian toxicity; CAUTION signal word for most formulations
  • Pyrethroids — synthetic versions; more persistent (permethrin, bifenthrin, lambda-cyhalothrin)
  • Your Riptide Pyrethrin ULV MGK contains pyrethrins + piperonyl butoxide (PBO) synergist
  • Apply in early morning or evening when mosquitoes are active and wind is low
  • Bee caution: avoid spraying blooming plants; bees not active at dawn/dusk

Larvicide: BTi (Mosquito Dunks)

  • Your Summit Mosquito Dunks contain Bacillus thuringiensis israelensis (BTi)
  • Kills mosquito and black fly larvae; safe for all other wildlife, fish, people, pets
  • Place in any standing water that can't be eliminated
  • One dunk treats 100 sq ft of standing water for 30 days
📌 For your exam: BTi is the safest larvicide. Pyrethrins are the least persistent pyrethroid-type adulticide. Label says "CAUTION" — least toxic signal word.

After Passing — Apply for Your License

Exam results are typically available online within a few days. When you pass both the Core and 7B:

  • Return to the portal to apply for your Commercial Pesticide Applicator License
  • Pay the $75 license fee
  • Your license is valid for 5 years; requires 12 CEUs to renew
  • Once licensed, you are legally authorized to apply pesticides for compensation in NC
